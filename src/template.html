In the fictional world of culinary folklore, the Rudubear Ajisen Ramen Restaurant has a history as rich and layered as its signature tonkotsu broth.

The Legend of the "Rudubear" (1962–1967)

Long before the first bowl was served, the story began in the snowy mountains of Hokkaido. Local legend tells of a mountain guide named Rudu, a man known for his rugged strength and surprisingly gentle spirit. One winter, while seeking shelter from a blizzard, Rudu shared his campfire and a humble pot of wheat noodles with a lost, orphaned bear cub.

The two became inseparable, and the villagers began calling them the "Rudubear duo." Rudu discovered that the cub had an uncanny "nose" for finding the best wild garlic and mountain spring water—the two secret ingredients that would eventually define their broth.

The Ajisen Connection (1968)

In 1968, Rudu travelled south to Kumamoto, where he met Takaharu Shigemitsu, the real-life founder of Ajisen Ramen. Shigemitsu was impressed by Rudu’s mountain-forged "Secret Garlic Oil" (Senmiyu).

They decided to collaborate, blending the traditional Kumamoto-style tonkotsu with Rudu’s wild-harvested inspirations. They named the venture Rudubear Ajisen to honor the "Thousand Flavours" (the meaning of Ajisen) and the bear that helped discover them.

Global Expansion & Modern Era

The restaurant became a cult classic, known for its unique mascot: a bear wearing a traditional hachimaki (headband) holding a giant ramen ladle.

1990s: The "Rudubear" brand became a symbol of warmth and endurance, opening its first international "den" in Hong Kong (1996).

2005: The legend crossed the Pacific, with Rudubear Ajisen opening its doors in Toronto as a pioneer of the Canadian ramen scene.

Today: The restaurant remains famous for its "1,800-Second Grind", a technique said to be inspired by the way the original bear cub used to patiently crush garlic cloves with its paws.
